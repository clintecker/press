# Writing Well

## Purpose

Create nonfiction that says something definite, rewards the reader's attention, and sounds like a person rather than an institution or a text generator.

This skill is an original, operational synthesis of the fundamental lessons associated with William Zinsser's *On Writing Well*. It applies those lessons as agent behavior; it does not reproduce the book or imitate its prose.

## Core standard

Good nonfiction should be:

- **Clear:** the reader can follow the thought on the first pass.
- **Economical:** every word performs useful work.
- **Unified:** the piece knows what it is about and stays about it.
- **Specific:** concrete facts, actions, people, and images replace abstraction.
- **Accurate:** simplification never distorts the truth.
- **Human:** the writer's judgment, curiosity, humor, and cadence remain present.
- **Alive:** each sentence gives the reader a reason to continue.

Clarity is not merely a surface style. It is evidence that the writer has decided what they mean.

## Operating principles

### 1. Find the point before polishing the prose

Before drafting or revising, determine:

- What is this really about?
- What is the one thing the reader should understand, feel, decide, or do?
- Why is this worth the reader's time?

If the central point cannot be stated in one plain sentence, the thinking is not ready. Resolve the thought before decorating the language.

### 2. Treat attention as scarce

The reader owes the writer nothing. Do not make the reader fight through throat-clearing, repetition, jargon, or background that has not yet become relevant.

Start where the energy begins. Supply context when the reader needs it, not merely when the writer learned it.

### 3. Establish unity

Choose a coherent set of decisions and defend them throughout the piece:

- one principal subject
- one controlling idea
- a deliberate scope
- a consistent point of view
- a stable tense unless time genuinely shifts
- a recognizable tone
- an appropriate level of technical detail

Unity does not mean monotony. It means every useful variation still serves the same piece.

### 4. Remove clutter without removing meaning

Cut words, phrases, sentences, anecdotes, and sections that do not advance the reader's understanding or experience.

Common clutter includes:

- ceremonial openings
- repeated conclusions
- inflated diction
- bureaucratic phrasing
- vague qualifiers
- redundant modifiers
- clichés
- needless transitions
- jargon used as camouflage
- abstractions that conceal the actor or action
- facts included only because they were expensive to obtain

Do not shorten mechanically. A necessary qualification, technical distinction, rhythm, joke, or image is not clutter.

### 5. Prefer strong nouns and verbs

Use concrete nouns and active verbs when they express the truth directly.

Prefer:

- the actor over an abstract process
- the action over a nominalization
- the specific object over a broad category
- the familiar word over the inflated synonym

Passive voice is allowed when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, obvious, or deliberately deemphasized. The goal is clarity, not grammatical dogma.

### 6. Preserve the writer's voice

Do not improve prose by sanding away everything distinctive.

Preserve, when useful:

- characteristic vocabulary
- sentence rhythm
- dry humor
- enthusiasm
- skepticism
- regional or professional texture
- first-person judgment
- controlled informality

Remove affectation, not personality. The final prose should sound like the writer on a particularly lucid day.

### 7. Be concrete

Replace general claims with observable evidence whenever possible:

- names
- numbers
- actions
- objects
- scenes
- sensory detail
- quotations from supplied sources
- small representative examples

Do not invent specificity. Use a visible placeholder or flag the missing fact.

### 8. Build a lead that creates forward motion

A lead should do at least one of these:

- reveal the central tension
- state a consequential fact
- place the reader in a scene
- ask a question the piece will genuinely answer
- make a precise claim
- introduce a person in motion

Avoid announcing the existence of the document. Open with the subject, not with "This article will discuss..."

The lead makes a promise. The rest of the piece must keep it.

### 9. Make paragraphs move

Each paragraph should have a job. It should develop, complicate, demonstrate, contrast, or advance the controlling idea.

Use transitions of thought, not decorative transition words. The relationship between paragraphs should be intelligible even when words such as *however*, *furthermore*, and *therefore* are removed.

End paragraphs on words with weight. Avoid burying the strongest idea in the middle.

### 10. End when the work is done

A good ending feels earned, not attached.

Possible endings:

- resolve the opening tension
- return to an image from the lead with changed meaning
- state the consequence
- identify the next action
- land on a revealing detail
- use a supplied quotation that adds finality

Do not summarize everything merely because the piece is ending. Avoid "In conclusion" unless the form explicitly requires it. When the point has landed, stop.

### 11. Rewrite as part of writing

Treat revision as the main craft, not as cleanup after the craft.

Revise in this order:

1. **Truth:** Are the claims supportable and accurately qualified?
2. **Purpose:** Is the central point worth making and clearly stated?
3. **Scope:** What belongs in this piece, and what belongs elsewhere?
4. **Structure:** Is the material in the most useful order?
5. **Movement:** Does each paragraph pull the reader forward?
6. **Clarity:** Can each sentence be understood on the first pass?
7. **Economy:** What can be removed without loss?
8. **Voice:** Does this still sound like the writer?
9. **Sound:** Does the prose read naturally aloud?
10. **Mechanics:** Grammar, punctuation, spelling, formatting, and citations.

Do not spend time perfecting sentences in a section that should be deleted.

## Task modes

Infer the task mode from the request. Do not force the user to name it.

### Draft

Create a complete piece from supplied facts and constraints.

- Determine the controlling idea.
- Choose a structure appropriate to the reader and purpose.
- Use only supported facts.
- Mark material unknowns with concise placeholders.
- Deliver finished prose, not a narration of the drafting process.

### Rewrite

Produce a replacement version of supplied prose.

- Preserve facts, intent, stance, names, numbers, and citations.
- Improve structure before line-level style.
- Keep the author's recognizable voice.
- Do not introduce new claims without permission or sourcing.

### Line edit

Improve sentence-level clarity, economy, rhythm, grammar, and diction while preserving the structure and meaning unless a local structural fix is unavoidable.

### Developmental edit

Diagnose and repair the controlling idea, scope, organization, pacing, evidence, lead, and ending. Rewrite sections when the request permits it.

### Critique

Prioritize the few changes that will matter most.

Discuss, in order:

1. central point
2. scope and unity
3. structure and movement
4. evidence and specificity
5. voice and tone
6. sentence-level clutter

Do not bury major structural problems beneath a long list of copy edits.

### Compress

Reduce length while preserving the full argument and necessary nuance.

Delete in this order:

1. repetition
2. throat-clearing
3. empty transitions
4. generic examples
5. secondary qualifications
6. background not needed for the central point

Never remove a caveat that changes the truth of the claim merely to hit a word count.

### Adapt

Rewrite for a new audience, medium, or level of expertise.

Preserve the underlying meaning while changing:

- assumed knowledge
- vocabulary
- detail
- pacing
- formality
- examples
- call to action

Do not confuse accessibility with condescension.

Before substantial drafting, silently establish:

```text

```

Ask a question only when a missing fact materially prevents an accurate or useful result. Otherwise make a reasonable, visible assumption or use a placeholder.

## Structural patterns

Choose the simplest structure that fits the material.

### Claim → evidence → consequence

Useful for arguments, analysis, and technical explanation.

### Problem → cause → response

Useful for incident reports, proposals, and operational writing.

### Scene → context → meaning

Useful for profiles, memoir, travel, and narrative nonfiction.

### Question → investigation → answer

Useful when the reader's curiosity is the organizing force.

### Chronology with selection

Useful when sequence matters. Include only events that change the reader's understanding.

### Comparison by criterion

Useful for evaluations. Compare alternatives against the same explicit criteria rather than describing each in isolation.

## Sentence-level editing rules

Apply judgment rather than a rigid style checker.

### Prefer direct constructions

| Cluttered | More direct |
|---|---|
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that | because |
| at this point in time | now |
| has the ability to | can |
| is in the process of reviewing | is reviewing |
| made the decision to leave | decided to leave |
| there are several teams that use it | several teams use it |
| it is important to note that the service failed | the service failed |
| an evaluation of the system was conducted by us | we evaluated the system |
| utilize, when no special distinction is intended | use |

These are diagnostic examples, not automatic substitutions.

### Watch for weak abstractions

Nominalizations often hide the action:

- "implementation of the change" may become "implement the change"
- "a reduction in latency" may become "latency fell"
- "the creation of a plan" may become "we made a plan"

Keep the noun form when the concept itself is the subject.

### Control modifiers

Use adjectives and adverbs when they add information that the noun or verb cannot carry alone. Remove them when they merely insist that something is important, innovative, unique, meaningful, powerful, or significant.

### Use qualifiers precisely

Distinguish:

- *may* from *will*
- *often* from *always*
- *associated with* from *caused by*
- *suggests* from *proves*

Delete hedges that reflect nervousness rather than uncertainty. Keep those required by the evidence.

### Vary rhythm naturally

Use sentence length to control pace and emphasis. A short sentence can land a point. A longer sentence can carry a layered thought. Neither is virtuous by itself.

Read difficult passages aloud. Revise wherever the voice trips, the emphasis lands on the wrong word, or the reader must backtrack.

## LLM-specific failure modes

Actively prevent these defaults:

### Generic throat-clearing

Do not begin with broad claims about the modern world, the importance of the topic, or the complexity of the landscape unless those claims are themselves the point and are supported.

### Institutional fog

Avoid language that sounds approved by a committee but means little: vague aspiration, empty strategy words, and claims without actors or consequences.

### Synthetic symmetry

Do not force every idea into three parallel bullets, repeated contrasts, or identical paragraph shapes. Use structure because the material calls for it.

### Excessive headings

Do not turn a short continuous argument into a stack of labeled fragments. Use headings when they help navigation, not because headings are easy to generate.

### Listification

Use prose when relationships among ideas matter. Use a list when the items are genuinely parallel or actionable.

### Repetitive recap

Do not restate the same point in the introduction, body, and conclusion with slightly different words.

### Inflated transitions

Avoid filler such as "It is worth noting," "It is important to understand," or "With that being said" when the next sentence can simply state the fact.

### False authority

Do not invent facts, sources, quotations, experiences, or consensus. Do not make a sentence more confident than the evidence permits.

### Voice erasure

Do not replace the user's plain, funny, sharp, technical, skeptical, or idiosyncratic voice with generic professional polish.

### Indiscriminate terseness

Do not cut the detail that gives a piece credibility, pleasure, rhythm, or emotional truth. Concision means no wasted motion, not minimum word count.

### Decorative profundity

Avoid ending with a vague universal lesson that the piece did not earn.

## Form-specific guidance

### Technical and science writing

- Explain the governing idea before secondary machinery.
- Define necessary terms once, near first use.
- Use a concrete example to anchor an abstraction.
- Preserve uncertainty, constraints, and causal distinctions.
- Write for an intelligent reader who may not share the author's specialty.
- Do not make the subject sound harder merely to establish expertise.

### Business writing

- Identify who did what, what changed, and what happens next.
- Put decisions and requests where they can be found.
- Replace mission-language with observable outcomes.
- Keep necessary dates, owners, costs, risks, and dependencies explicit.
- Respect the reader's time without stripping away context needed for a decision.

### Interviews and profiles

- Prepare enough to ask informed questions, then listen for surprise.
- Seek specific memories, actions, choices, and language.
- Let supplied quotations reveal character rather than merely confirm exposition.
- Verify quotations and factual details.
- The writer shapes the portrait but should not counterfeit the subject.

### Memoir and personal nonfiction

- Select a manageable slice of life rather than attempting an entire life.
- Use remembered detail to create a scene, while distinguishing memory from documented fact.
- Let the adult narrator interpret without smothering the earlier self.
- Prefer honest complexity to a tidy moral.

### Travel and place writing

- Focus on what is distinctive about the place and the writer's encounter with it.
- Avoid cataloging attractions.
- Use people, customs, work, language, landscape, and small observed details.
- Do not turn residents into scenery.

### Criticism and reviews

- State the criteria of judgment, explicitly or through the analysis.
- Describe enough of the work for the judgment to be intelligible.
- Support praise and criticism with particulars.
- Be fair to the work actually attempted, not the work the critic wishes had been attempted.

## Editing examples

These examples are original and illustrate the method.

### Remove process language

**Before**

> At this point in time, the infrastructure team is in the process of conducting an evaluation of several possible database solutions.

**After**

> The infrastructure team is evaluating several databases.

### Replace abstraction with action

**Before**

> The deployment resulted in an improvement in the reduction of request latency.

**After**

> The deployment cut request latency.

### Make the actor visible

**Before**

> A decision was made to postpone the launch after several issues were identified.

**After**

> The release team postponed the launch after tests exposed three data-loss bugs.

Use the more specific version only when those details are supplied or verified.

### Start with the subject

**Before**

> This report will provide an overview of the outage that occurred on Tuesday and discuss the lessons that can be learned from it.

**After**

> On Tuesday, an expired certificate blocked checkout for 18 minutes.

### Preserve voice while cleaning clutter

**Before**

> To be honest, the new build system is, in my personal opinion, an extremely clever solution that is also kind of a nightmare to debug.

**After**

> The new build system is clever and a nightmare to debug.

The opinion and cadence survive; the scaffolding does not.

### End on consequence

**Weak ending**

> In conclusion, the migration had several challenges, but the team learned many valuable lessons that will be useful in the future.

**Stronger ending**

> The next migration will begin with a rollback test, not end with one.

## Output behavior

### For finished prose

- Put the usable text first.
- Match the requested format and length.
- Do not append a generic summary of what was changed.
- Add edit notes only when they help the user make a decision or when requested.

### For critiques

- Lead with the highest-leverage diagnosis.
- Quote or reference only enough text to locate the problem.
- Explain the effect on the reader.
- Give a concrete repair.
- Distinguish structural issues from optional style preferences.

### For ambiguous source text

- Preserve the ambiguity when it may be intentional.
- Flag it when it threatens accuracy.
- Do not silently choose a new meaning.

### For required terminology

Legal, scientific, regulatory, and technical terms may be irreducible. Keep them when precision requires them, then make the surrounding prose clear.

## Final revision checklist

Before delivering, verify:

### Meaning

- Can the controlling idea be stated in one sentence?
- Does every major section serve it?
- Are claims accurate and properly qualified?
- Did any edit change the intended meaning?

### Structure

- Does the lead begin where the reader's interest begins?
- Is information presented when the reader needs it?
- Does each paragraph have a clear job?
- Are transitions based on thought rather than filler?
- Does the ending stop at the right moment?

### Language

- Can a reader understand each sentence on the first pass?
- Is every word doing useful work?
- Are actors and actions visible where they matter?
- Are nouns and verbs carrying most of the sentence?
- Are examples concrete and supported?
- Are jargon and abbreviations necessary and explained?

### Voice

- Does the prose sound like a particular human being?
- Has useful humor, judgment, warmth, or skepticism survived?
- Did editing remove affectation without removing personality?
- Does the rhythm sound natural aloud?

### Reader

- Is the assumed knowledge appropriate?
- Have objections or questions been answered at the right point?
- Is the requested action or takeaway unmistakable?
- Has the piece earned the reader's time?

## Compact scoring rubric

Score each category from 0 to 2.

| Category | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Point | unclear | present but diffuse | precise and consequential |
| Unity | fragmented | mostly coherent | every part serves the whole |
| Structure | difficult to follow | workable | inevitable and propulsive |
| Clarity | frequent rereading | occasional friction | first-pass comprehension |
| Economy | heavily padded | some clutter | no wasted motion |
| Specificity | mostly abstract | mixed | concrete and credible |
| Voice | generic or affected | intermittent | distinctive and natural |
| Accuracy | unsupported or distorted | minor uncertainty | supported and well-qualified |
| Lead | ceremonial or vague | functional | creates immediate interest |
| Ending | repetitive or attached | adequate | earned and decisive |

Revise any category scored 0. A score of 1 may be acceptable when required by the form, evidence, or audience; otherwise improve it.

## Governing rule

Say what you mean. Make it easy to follow. Keep what gives it life. Remove everything else.
