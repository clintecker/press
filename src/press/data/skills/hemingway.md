# Hemingway-Inspired Prose

## Purpose

Use this skill to produce prose that is direct, concrete, restrained, and emotionally weighted without becoming a surface imitation of Ernest Hemingway.

The goal is not to copy famous sentences, recurring characters, historical settings, or recognizable verbal tics. The goal is to apply the deeper craft lessons that recur across Hemingway’s novels, stories, journalism, and memoirs:

- Say only what the reader needs.
- Make physical facts carry emotional meaning.
- Let important material remain partly unstated.
- Prefer exact nouns and active verbs to explanation.
- Build pressure through action, dialogue, silence, and consequence.
- Keep the prose simple without making the thought simplistic.
- End before the meaning has been fully explained.

## Use This Skill When

Apply this skill to:

- Short stories and literary fiction
- Memoir and personal essays
- Scene-based journalism
- Travel, nature, war, sport, or adventure writing
- Dialogue-heavy scenes
- Prose that feels overwritten, sentimental, vague, or inert
- Revision requests asking for greater clarity, force, restraint, or compression

Do not apply it automatically to:

- Technical documentation
- Legal language
- Academic argument
- Marketing copy that requires explicit persuasion
- Expository writing where omission would reduce accuracy
- Work whose existing voice depends on lush, comic, baroque, or highly interior prose

## Core Doctrine

### 1. Begin with the truest observable thing

Anchor the passage in what can be seen, heard, touched, carried, broken, eaten, lost, or done.

Replace generalized atmosphere with specific evidence.

Weak:

> The room felt tense and everyone was nervous.

Stronger:

> No one took off a coat. The coffee went cold between them.

The stronger version does not name the emotion. It gives the reader enough evidence to feel it.

### 2. Put the weight below the surface

The visible scene should be only the exposed part of the story.

A character may discuss weather, money, travel, food, work, or practical arrangements while the real subject is grief, fear, loyalty, shame, love, mortality, or betrayal.

Do not conceal essential plot facts merely to appear subtle. Omit explanation, not causality.

The reader should be able to sense the submerged meaning from:

- Repeated objects or gestures
- Avoided questions
- Contradictory statements
- Changes in routine
- Practical decisions with emotional cost
- What a character notices but does not discuss
- What a character refuses to notice

### 3. Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs

Use the most exact ordinary word available.

Prefer:

- `walked` over `proceeded`
- `held` over `maintained possession of`
- `rain` over `inclement weather`
- `knife` over `cutting instrument`

Avoid decorating a weak noun with adjectives when a stronger noun exists.

Weak:

> He carried a very large, old, heavy bag.

Stronger:

> He dragged the canvas duffel.

### 4. Make emotion visible through behavior

Do not announce a feeling when an action can reveal it.

Instead of:

> She was devastated by his departure.

Try:

> She washed his cup and put it back beside hers.

Use direct emotional language only when the plain statement is itself the strongest choice. Restraint is not a ban on naming feelings. It is a ban on explaining them after the scene has already made them clear.

### 5. Keep sentences clear, then vary their pressure

Use simple sentence structures as the base. Vary length deliberately.

Short sentences can:

- Establish fact
- Deliver consequence
- End debate
- Mark danger
- Create finality

Longer sentences can:

- Accumulate motion
- Track observation
- Delay an outcome
- Reproduce fatigue, distance, or thought under pressure

Do not make every sentence short. Uniform brevity becomes parody.

### 6. Use repetition with purpose

Repeat plain words when repetition adds rhythm, insistence, memory, dread, or inevitability.

Do not replace every repeated word with a synonym merely for variety. Exact repetition can be stronger than decorative variation.

Remove repetition that only restates meaning.

### 7. Let dialogue perform several jobs at once

Good dialogue should reveal at least two of the following:

- What the speaker wants
- What the speaker fears
- What the speaker refuses to say
- The relationship between the speakers
- A change in power
- A practical decision
- A threat, promise, test, or retreat

Keep dialogue plain enough to sound spoken. Let pauses, evasions, interruptions, and repeated phrases carry subtext.

Avoid explaining the subtext immediately afterward.

Weak:

> “You can leave,” she said, though secretly she hoped he would stay.

Stronger:

> “You can leave,” she said.
>
> He picked up his coat.
>
> “It’s still raining,” she said.

### 8. Treat setting as resistance

Landscape and weather should not merely decorate the scene. They should alter what characters can do.

The river rises. The road freezes. The sun exposes them. The wind takes the words away. The room is too hot to sleep. The distance costs time.

Physical conditions create pressure, and pressure reveals character.

### 9. Write competence precisely

When a character fishes, cooks, boxes, repairs an engine, cleans a wound, carries a rifle, rows a boat, or pours a drink, describe the work accurately enough to earn trust.

Use procedural detail selectively. Include the step that reveals danger, skill, fatigue, ritual, or character. Omit the rest.

Competence is most powerful when the character’s method reveals what is at stake.

### 10. Show dignity through conduct

Characters become meaningful through what they do when circumstances cannot be fixed.

Useful thematic pressures include:

- Endurance without certainty
- Courage without spectacle
- Loyalty without reward
- Work performed despite loss
- Grace under pressure
- The difference between defeat and surrender

Do not force every story into heroic stoicism. These are available pressures, not mandatory morals.

### 11. Remove the sentence that explains the scene

After drafting, find the line that tells the reader what the passage means.

Delete it.

Read the scene again. Restore the line only if the meaning becomes genuinely inaccessible rather than merely unstated.

### 12. Stop at the point of resonance

End the scene when the action, image, or final exchange has created enough pressure to continue in the reader’s mind.

Avoid:

- Moral summaries
- Emotional recaps
- Theme statements
- Explanations of symbolism
- A final paragraph that repeats the ending

## Drafting Procedure

### Step 1: Identify the hard fact

State privately, in one sentence, what actually happens.

Examples:

- A son asks his father to sell the boat.
- Two people wait for medical results and discuss lunch.
- A soldier chooses whether to carry a wounded friend.
- A woman packs after deciding not to return.

This sentence is the event, not the theme.

### Step 2: Identify the submerged subject

State privately what the scene is really about.

Examples:

- Aging
- Fear of abandonment
- Moral debt
- The end of a marriage
- Shame after failure
- Love expressed as practical care

Do not insert this statement directly into the prose unless necessary.

### Step 3: Choose a physical anchor

Select one recurring concrete element:

- A cup
- A wound
- A broken zipper
- A fish line
- A train ticket
- A wet coat
- A dull knife
- A watch that has stopped

Let the object participate in the action. Do not turn it into an announced symbol.

### Step 4: Draft the scene plainly

Write what happens in chronological order.

Favor:

- Action
- Observation
- Dialogue
- Physical consequence

Delay interpretation.

### Step 5: Compress

Cut:

- Explanations already implied
- Decorative adjectives
- Adverbs that repeat the verb
- Stage directions the dialogue makes obvious
- Backstory that does not alter the present action
- Metaphors that call attention to themselves
- Sentences written only to prove the author can write

### Step 6: Restore necessary depth

Compression can remove too much. Restore any detail needed for:

- Causality
- Spatial clarity
- Character motivation
- Emotional contrast
- Rhythm
- The reader’s trust

Sparse prose still needs a complete scene.

### Step 7: Read aloud

Listen for:

- False grandeur
- Monotony
- Accidental rhyme
- Too many identical sentence openings
- Repetition without pressure
- Dialogue no person would say
- A final sentence that explains instead of lands

## Sentence-Level Rules

Use these as defaults, not rigid laws.

- Prefer active voice when the actor matters.
- Use passive voice when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately concealed.
- Keep modifiers close to what they modify.
- Favor one strong image over several approximate images.
- Use adverbs sparingly, especially after dialogue tags.
- Prefer `said` to theatrical dialogue tags.
- Use conjunctions to sustain motion, not as a costume.
- Break a long sentence when its logic becomes harder to follow than its action.
- Keep short sentences for moments that deserve impact.
- Do not mistake grammatical simplicity for intellectual simplicity.

## Description Rules

Describe only what the viewpoint character would notice under the circumstances.

A frightened person notices exits, hands, distance, sound, and delay.

A hungry person notices portions, smell, grease, and waste.

An experienced sailor notices wind, line tension, cloud shape, and current.

Description should answer at least one question:

- What can happen here?
- What has happened here?
- What does the character need?
- What is changing?
- What is dangerous?
- What does the character refuse to look at?

## Dialogue Rules

- Enter dialogue late.
- Leave dialogue early.
- Let speakers answer indirectly when directness would cost them something.
- Use silence as an action, not an empty pause.
- Give each speaker a distinct objective rather than a decorative voice.
- Avoid speeches unless the speaker has earned the pressure to make one.
- Do not use dialogue to deliver information both speakers already know.
- Trust the reader to infer the emotional subject.

## Revision Passes

### The Abstraction Pass

Circle words such as:

- sadness
- tension
- beauty
- trauma
- destiny
- connection
- darkness
- courage
- pain
- freedom

For each one, ask whether a physical fact can carry the meaning instead.

### The Verb Pass

Replace weak verb-plus-adverb combinations with a more exact verb.

Weak:

> He walked slowly and painfully.

Stronger:

> He limped.

Do not force a vivid verb where a plain verb is more truthful.

### The Explanation Pass

Delete sentences beginning with ideas like:

- This showed that...
- She realized that...
- He felt as though...
- What this meant was...
- In a way...
- The reason was...

Restore only what the scene cannot communicate by itself.

### The Adjective Pass

For every adjective, ask:

- Is it necessary for identification?
- Does it alter the action?
- Is it more precise than the noun alone?
- Is it merely asking the reader to feel something?

Keep exact adjectives. Cut emotional instructions.

### The Dialogue Pass

For every line, identify:

- The literal meaning
- The speaker’s objective
- The risk of saying it
- The more important thing left unsaid

If all four are identical, the dialogue may be too explicit.

### The Ending Pass

Test the final paragraph by removing it.

If the piece becomes stronger, leave it out.

If the piece becomes confusing, rewrite the paragraph so it completes the action without explaining the theme.

## Failure Modes

### Telegraphic parody

Bad imitation often becomes a sequence of tiny declarative sentences.

> It was cold. He was tired. The coffee was bad. He drank it.

This is not automatically forceful. Sentence length must follow pressure and rhythm.

### Decorative toughness

Do not add guns, whiskey, blood, war, hunting, boxing, or emotional silence merely to create a “Hemingway” atmosphere.

Use only details the story has earned.

### Empty stoicism

A character who never speaks or reflects is not necessarily restrained. Give silence a motive and a cost.

### Fake profundity

Plain language does not make a vague statement profound.

Weak:

> The night was dark and he knew all men were alone.

Prefer an exact event with consequences.

### Withholding necessary information

Omission should create depth, not confusion. The reader may be uncertain about meaning, but should usually understand what physically occurred.

### Excessive symbolism

Objects gain meaning through recurrence and use. Do not label them as symbols.

### Surface mimicry

Do not copy:

- Famous opening or closing structures
- Signature phrases
- Recognizable scenes
- Recurrent Hemingway protagonists
- Period dialogue used only for effect
- Biographical details imported into unrelated work

## Output Requirements

When this skill is active:

1. Preserve the user’s intended meaning, facts, and point of view.
2. Use plain, precise language.
3. Favor scene, action, and concrete evidence over commentary.
4. Keep emotion controlled but legible.
5. Vary sentence length according to pressure.
6. Remove unnecessary explanation.
7. Avoid parody, pastiche, and copied phrasing.
8. Do not mention Hemingway in the output unless the user asks for analysis.
9. Do not make every character stoic, masculine, violent, or emotionally unavailable.
10. Return the finished prose without a craft lecture unless commentary is requested.

## Compact Working Prompt

Use this internal prompt when applying the skill:

> Write the scene in plain, exact prose. Begin with observable action. Use concrete nouns and active verbs. Let gesture, dialogue, silence, setting, and consequence carry the emotional weight. Keep the central meaning partly submerged but preserve causal clarity. Vary sentence length with the pressure of the scene. Cut decorative language, redundant explanation, and any ending that tells the reader what to feel. Avoid copying recognizable Hemingway phrases, scenes, or mannerisms.

## Final Checklist

Before returning the work, verify:

- [ ] The physical action is clear.
- [ ] The emotional subject is present without being overexplained.
- [ ] Concrete details do real narrative work.
- [ ] Verbs are active and exact where appropriate.
- [ ] Adjectives and adverbs have survived scrutiny.
- [ ] Dialogue contains pressure or subtext.
- [ ] Sentence lengths vary with rhythm and consequence.
- [ ] Omission creates depth rather than confusion.
- [ ] The prose avoids macho caricature and surface imitation.
- [ ] The ending stops before explanation takes over.
