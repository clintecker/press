# Elements of Style

## Purpose

Use this skill to make prose easier to understand and harder to misread.

The goal is not to make every writer sound the same. Preserve the writer's meaning, voice, level of formality, and intended rhythm while removing confusion, slack, clutter, and accidental ambiguity.

This skill treats style as a practical discipline:

- say exactly what is meant;
- arrange ideas in a useful order;
- prefer strong grammatical structures;
- remove words that do no work;
- choose concrete language when possible;
- preserve deliberate exceptions.

## Use This Skill When

Apply this skill when asked to:

- edit, revise, polish, tighten, or simplify prose;
- improve clarity, flow, grammar, or readability;
- make writing more direct, professional, persuasive, or concise;
- diagnose why a passage feels vague, weak, repetitive, or awkward;
- turn a rough draft into finished prose;
- establish or enforce a plain-language house style.

It works well for:

- essays and reports;
- technical documentation;
- product and design writing;
- emails and memos;
- instructions and policies;
- speeches and presentations;
- nonfiction prose;
- explanatory writing.

Use more cautiously for poetry, dialogue, fiction, advertising, dialect, legal language, or experimental prose. In those forms, a fragment, repetition, passive construction, unusual syntax, or ornate phrase may be intentional.

## Core Doctrine

### 1. Preserve the intended meaning

Never improve style by changing the claim.

Before revising, determine:

- what the passage is trying to say;
- what action, belief, or understanding it should produce;
- which qualifications are essential;
- which terms have technical or legal meaning;
- which unusual choices are deliberate.

Do not invent facts, implications, certainty, evidence, or emotional intent.

### 2. Make the sentence's structure visible

A reader should be able to find the sentence's main actor and action quickly.

Prefer:

- a concrete subject;
- a strong finite verb;
- a clear object or complement;
- modifiers placed near what they modify.

Weak:

> A review of the proposal was conducted by the committee.

Stronger:

> The committee reviewed the proposal.

The passive voice is not forbidden. Use it when the receiver matters more than the actor, the actor is unknown, or the surrounding discourse already centers the receiver.

Useful passive:

> The samples were stored at −80°C.

### 3. Put important material where readers notice it

Readers tend to notice beginnings and endings.

Use the beginning of a sentence or paragraph to establish context. Use the end for the point that deserves emphasis.

Less forceful:

> We rejected the design because it could not recover safely after a partial failure.

More emphatic:

> Because it could not recover safely after a partial failure, we rejected the design.

Do not mechanically move every important phrase to the end. Choose an order that makes the reasoning easy to follow.

### 4. Prefer verbs that express the action

Avoid burying actions inside nouns when a direct verb is available.

Weak:

> The team made a decision to perform an evaluation of the service.

Stronger:

> The team decided to evaluate the service.

Common nominalizations include words ending in:

- `-tion`;
- `-ment`;
- `-ance`;
- `-ity`;
- `-al`.

Do not remove a nominalization when the noun itself is the subject under discussion.

### 5. Use concrete, specific language

Prefer words that let the reader picture, test, count, or verify the claim.

Vague:

> The system sometimes has performance issues under significant load.

Specific:

> At 500 concurrent requests, median latency rises from 80 ms to 340 ms.

When exact details are unavailable, retain honest uncertainty rather than fabricating precision.

### 6. Remove words that do no work

Delete words, phrases, and sentences that repeat, delay, inflate, or obscure the point.

Common clutter:

- throat-clearing introductions;
- redundant pairs;
- obvious transitions;
- empty intensifiers;
- repeated qualifications;
- unnecessary restatements;
- meta-commentary about the writing itself.

Cluttered:

> It is important to note that the reason the release was delayed was due to the fact that the tests were failing.

Direct:

> The release was delayed because the tests failed.

Conciseness is not mere shortness. Keep words that add accuracy, rhythm, emphasis, courtesy, or necessary context.

### 7. State claims directly

Prefer positive, definite statements when they accurately express the idea.

Indirect:

> The proposal is not without merit.

Direct:

> The proposal has merit.

Use negation when the contrast itself matters.

Necessary contrast:

> The change reduces memory use, not execution time.

### 8. Keep related words together

A modifier should sit close to the word or phrase it modifies.

Ambiguous:

> She nearly reviewed every pull request.

This says she almost reviewed them but did not.

Clear:

> She reviewed nearly every pull request.

Also watch for dangling introductory phrases.

Dangling:

> After reviewing the logs, the cause was obvious.

Revised:

> After reviewing the logs, we found the cause.

### 9. Use parallel form for parallel ideas

Items that perform the same grammatical job should use the same structure.

Uneven:

> The service is fast, reliable, and deploys easily.

Parallel:

> The service is fast, reliable, and easy to deploy.

Apply parallelism to:

- lists;
- comparisons;
- paired conjunctions;
- headings;
- repeated sentence patterns;
- procedural steps.

### 10. Keep tense, person, and point of view stable

Shift only when the subject or timeline requires it.

Unstable:

> The user opens the settings page and clicked Save.

Stable:

> The user opens the settings page and clicks Save.

Likewise, avoid moving unpredictably among `I`, `we`, `you`, `the user`, and impersonal constructions.

### 11. Make each paragraph do one main job

A paragraph should advance one unit of thought.

A strong paragraph usually contains:

1. a controlling idea;
2. evidence, explanation, or development;
3. a useful conclusion or transition.

Split a paragraph when it changes subject, time, place, speaker, argumentative step, or level of detail.

Combine paragraphs when they are fragments of the same thought and the separation adds no useful emphasis.

### 12. Use transitions to express real relationships

Transitions should name the logical connection between ideas, not merely decorate the prose.

Useful relationships include:

- cause;
- contrast;
- sequence;
- example;
- qualification;
- consequence;
- summary.

Do not begin every paragraph with a mechanical connector. Often the order of the ideas supplies enough continuity.

### 13. Prefer familiar words over inflated ones

Choose the ordinary word when it is equally precise.

Inflated:

> Utilize the interface to initiate the configuration process.

Plain:

> Use the interface to configure the service.

Retain specialized language when writing for specialists or when no ordinary word carries the same meaning.

### 14. Avoid vague references

Pronouns and placeholders must have obvious antecedents.

Vague:

> When the client sends the server a token, it validates it.

Clear:

> When the client sends a token, the server validates the token.

Repeated nouns are preferable to ambiguous pronouns.

### 15. Treat punctuation as structure

Use punctuation to reveal relationships, not to make a sentence look sophisticated.

General guidance:

- use a comma to separate introductory material when it improves readability;
- use commas to separate items in a series;
- use a semicolon only between closely related independent clauses or in complex lists;
- use a colon after a complete clause to introduce an explanation, list, or consequence;
- use dashes sparingly for interruption or emphasis;
- do not join independent clauses with a comma alone;
- avoid apostrophes in ordinary plurals;
- make restrictive and nonrestrictive clauses unambiguous.

When punctuation becomes complicated, consider rewriting the sentence instead.

### 16. Prefer one strong sentence over several weak fragments

Combine short related statements when the relationship becomes clearer.

Choppy:

> The migration failed. The schema was incompatible. We restored the backup.

Connected:

> The migration failed because the schema was incompatible, so we restored the backup.

Keep short sentences when they create useful emphasis, pace, or contrast.

### 17. Break long sentences at conceptual joints

A long sentence is acceptable when its structure is easy to follow. A short sentence can still be confusing.

Split a sentence when it contains:

- multiple competing main clauses;
- more than one major turn in the reasoning;
- a long interruption between subject and verb;
- a list that obscures the main claim;
- nested qualifications that readers must hold in memory.

Do not split merely to satisfy an arbitrary word count.

### 18. Preserve deliberate voice

Do not flatten vivid, funny, strange, regional, technical, or personal writing into generic corporate prose.

Protect:

- characteristic diction;
- intentional rhythm;
- useful repetition;
- controlled informality;
- memorable images;
- culturally meaningful language;
- purposeful fragments.

Correct accidental weakness, not intentional personality.

## Revision Workflow

Perform the following passes in order. Do not begin with comma-level edits if the argument or structure is broken.

### Pass 1: Identify the assignment

Infer or determine:

- audience;
- purpose;
- desired action or effect;
- genre;
- level of formality;
- length constraints;
- facts and qualifications that must remain.

When the request is sufficiently specified, proceed without asking questions. Use visible placeholders only for genuinely missing facts that cannot be inferred.

### Pass 2: Find the spine

Summarize the passage in one sentence.

Then ask:

- Does every paragraph support that sentence?
- Is the main point easy to find?
- Does the order match the reader's needs?
- Is anything essential missing?
- Is anything present only because the writer happened to think of it?

Reorder, combine, or remove material before polishing sentences.

### Pass 3: Repair paragraphs

For each paragraph:

1. identify its main job;
2. move the controlling idea to a useful position;
3. remove unrelated material;
4. arrange support in a logical sequence;
5. add a transition only when the relationship is not already clear.

### Pass 4: Strengthen sentences

For each sentence:

1. locate the grammatical subject and main verb;
2. replace weak constructions with direct ones where appropriate;
3. convert nominalizations into verbs when that improves clarity;
4. place modifiers next to what they modify;
5. repair faulty coordination and subordination;
6. make parallel ideas parallel;
7. break or combine sentences at conceptual joints;
8. check pronoun references.

### Pass 5: Tighten words

Remove or replace:

- empty openings such as `there is`, `there are`, and `it is` when they conceal the subject;
- weak intensifiers such as `very`, `really`, `quite`, and `extremely` when a stronger word will do;
- redundant phrases such as `future plans`, `basic fundamentals`, and `end result`;
- inflated phrases such as `due to the fact that` when `because` is enough;
- needless hedges that do not reflect real uncertainty;
- jargon that does not serve the audience;
- repeated information.

Do not delete necessary politeness, uncertainty, scope, safety language, or legal qualification.

### Pass 6: Check correctness

Verify:

- agreement;
- tense;
- pronoun case and reference;
- punctuation;
- capitalization;
- spelling;
- parallel structure;
- list consistency;
- numbers, units, and dates;
- quotation boundaries;
- formatting consistency.

Do not silently “correct” a technical term, proper noun, command, identifier, API name, or quoted string unless clearly wrong.

### Pass 7: Read for sound

Read the passage as spoken language.

Listen for:

- monotonous sentence openings;
- accidental repetition;
- awkward stress;
- breathless length;
- choppy rhythm;
- weak endings;
- unintended rhyme or alliteration;
- a tone that no longer matches the writer.

Vary sentence length and structure only when the variation improves comprehension or rhythm.

### Pass 8: Compare meaning

Before returning the revision, compare it with the source.

Confirm that the revision preserves:

- factual content;
- degree of certainty;
- chronology;
- causal relationships;
- speaker attitude;
- requested tone;
- required terminology;
- intended audience.

If a sentence is ambiguous and multiple readings are plausible, do not choose one silently. Preserve the ambiguity or flag it.

## Diagnostic Patterns

Use these as signals, not absolute prohibitions.

### Empty opening

Pattern:

> There are several reasons why the test failed.

Revision:

> The test failed for several reasons.

### Buried action

Pattern:

> We performed an analysis of the trace.

Revision:

> We analyzed the trace.

### Weak verb plus adjective

Pattern:

> The result was very surprising.

Revision:

> The result was startling.

### Double qualification

Pattern:

> It may possibly be the case that the cache is stale.

Revision:

> The cache may be stale.

### Redundant explanation

Pattern:

> The two alternatives are mutually exclusive and cannot both occur at the same time.

Revision:

> The alternatives are mutually exclusive.

### Vague actor

Pattern:

> Mistakes were made during deployment.

Revision when the actor is known:

> We misconfigured the deployment.

Keep the original passive form when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or intentionally withheld.

### False precision

Pattern:

> Most users prefer the new workflow.

Revision when evidence exists:

> In the June survey, 68% of respondents preferred the new workflow.

Revision when evidence does not exist:

> Early feedback favors the new workflow.

### Stacked nouns

Pattern:

> customer identity access policy configuration

Possible revision:

> configuration for the customer identity access policy

Choose the grouping that matches the domain meaning.

### Long subject delay

Pattern:

> The proposal, after several months of review by legal, security, finance, and the executive team, was approved.

Revision:

> After several months of review by legal, security, finance, and the executive team, the proposal was approved.

Or, when the reviewers are the important actors:

> Legal, security, finance, and the executive team reviewed the proposal for several months and approved it.

## Exceptions and Judgment

Style rules are tools, not laws.

### Passive voice

Use the passive voice when:

- the actor is unknown;
- the actor is irrelevant;
- the receiver is already the topic;
- the procedure matters more than the operator;
- naming the actor would distract or mislead.

### Repetition

Keep repetition when it:

- creates emphasis;
- clarifies parallel ideas;
- aids memory;
- establishes rhythm;
- prevents ambiguity.

### Fragments

Keep or create a fragment when it is deliberate, legible, and appropriate to the form.

> One more failure. Then rollback.

### Long words and technical terms

Use the precise term, even if unfamiliar, when precision matters. Define it when the audience may not know it.

### Long sentences

Keep a long sentence when its hierarchy is clear and its cadence supports the thought.

### Formality

Plain language does not require casual language. A direct sentence can still be formal, diplomatic, solemn, or technical.

## Context-Specific Guidance

### Technical documentation

Prioritize:

- explicit actors;
- exact conditions;
- stable terminology;
- ordered steps;
- commands that match the interface;
- clear distinction between requirement, recommendation, and example;
- warnings placed before the risky action.

Do not replace domain terms merely to sound simpler.

### Product and design writing

State:

- the user problem;
- the observed evidence;
- the decision;
- the tradeoff;
- the consequence.

Avoid abstract claims such as “improves experience” when the actual improvement can be named.

### Email and workplace writing

Lead with the purpose. Make the requested action and deadline easy to find. Preserve courtesy, but remove ceremonial padding.

### Academic writing

Retain necessary qualifications, citations, and disciplinary terms. Reduce inflated phrasing without overstating certainty.

### Legal and policy writing

Do not simplify away defined terms, exceptions, scope, conditions, or obligations. Clarity is important, but semantic precision takes priority over elegance.

### Narrative nonfiction

Protect scene, voice, pacing, imagery, and suspense. Apply compression where it sharpens the experience, not where it removes texture.

## Output Behavior

Unless the user asks for commentary:

- return the finished revision;
- do not narrate every edit;
- do not append generic writing advice;
- do not mention this skill;
- do not add facts or examples not present in the source;
- preserve formatting unless changing it improves comprehension.

When the user asks for an explanation, organize feedback by impact:

1. meaning or factual risk;
2. structural problems;
3. sentence-level clarity;
4. concision;
5. grammar and punctuation;
6. rhythm and tone.

When useful, show a small number of representative before-and-after examples rather than cataloging every mechanical change.

## Final Checklist

Before returning the result, ask:

- Is the main point obvious?
- Does the order fit the reader's needs?
- Does each paragraph have one main job?
- Can the reader quickly find each sentence's actor and action?
- Are important claims concrete and properly qualified?
- Are related words close together?
- Are lists and comparisons parallel?
- Are pronoun references unambiguous?
- Can any word be removed without losing meaning, precision, rhythm, or courtesy?
- Does punctuation reveal the structure?
- Did the revision preserve the writer's voice?
- Did the revision preserve the original meaning and degree of certainty?

If the answer to the last question is uncertain, revise again.
